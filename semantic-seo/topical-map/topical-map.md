# Semantic Topical Map Builder

Build complete semantic topical maps following Koray Tugberk Gubur's methodology. A topical map is NOT a simple content calendar — it's a strategic blueprint for an entire information domain that tells search engines you are THE authority.

## Core Principle

**Topical Authority = Topical Coverage × Historical Data**

Three pillars drive authority:
- **Vastness** — cover a wide range of related topics
- **Depth** — process topics with extreme detail
- **Momentum** — strategic publication frequency

The Compounding Threshold: above 60% semantic coverage of a topic, Google treats you as the default authority. New content indexes faster, old content ranks higher, and you rank for keywords you didn't even target. 15–20 strategic posts beat 50 random ones. Architecture matters more than volume.

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Define Source Context

Before mapping anything, establish the business identity. Source context dictates how every topic is framed.

Ask and document:
- How does the business make money? (e-commerce, SaaS, agency, consulting, etc.)
- What is the brand's unique identity and perspective?
- How does the brand connect to the central topic?
- What is the brand's "source context" — the lens through which all content is written?

This matters because an SEO agency, a SaaS tool, and a freelance consultant would all approach "technical SEO" from completely different angles. The source context shapes the entire map.

### Step 2: Select Central Entity

Identify the primary subject that appears throughout the entire map. This is the entity the site wants to be recognized as the authority on.

Examples:
- For an SEO agency: "SEO services" or "search engine optimization"
- For an email marketing SaaS: "email marketing software"
- For a fitness brand: "strength training"

The central entity must be specific enough to own but broad enough to build a full topical ecosystem around.

### Step 3: Define Central Search Intent

Identify the overarching purpose or "search language" reflected across all content. This is the intent thread that connects every piece of content on the site.

Examples:
- A visa site: "No and Go" (quick visa solutions)
- An SEO agency: "grow organic traffic and revenue"
- A SaaS tool: "simplify [workflow]"

### Step 4: Map the Topical Hierarchy

Build the full topic tree. Structure it as:

```
CENTRAL ENTITY
├── Core Section (monetization focus — deep attributes of the central entity)
│   ├── Parent Theme 1
│   │   ├── Sub-topic 1a
│   │   ├── Sub-topic 1b
│   │   └── Sub-topic 1c
│   ├── Parent Theme 2
│   │   ├── Sub-topic 2a
│   │   └── Sub-topic 2b
│   └── Parent Theme 3
│       └── ...
├── Outer Section (broader relevance — builds historical data and topical breadth)
│   ├── Parent Theme A
│   │   ├── Sub-topic Aa
│   │   └── Sub-topic Ab
│   └── Parent Theme B
│       └── ...
└── Contextual Bridges (connections between Core and Outer sections)
```

**Core Section** = where the money is. Deep coverage of the central entity's primary attributes. This is where pillar pages and BOFU content live.

**Outer Section** = broader topical relevance. Proves expertise on related concepts. Builds the historical data and breadth that compounds over time.

**Contextual Bridges** = the connections that make every outer topic link back to the core. Without bridges, outer content floats disconnected and doesn't contribute to authority.

### Step 5: Query Network Mapping

For each parent theme, map the full query ecosystem:

- **Query paths** — what users search before and after this query (the search journey)
- **Correlative queries** — queries frequently searched together in the same session
- **Query templates** — repeating patterns like:
  - "best [product] for [use case]"
  - "how to [action] with [tool]"
  - "[brand] vs [competitor]"
  - "[topic] for [industry/audience]"
  - "what is [concept]"

Use web search, SERP analysis, People Also Ask, and competitor research to build evidence-based query networks — not assumptions.

### Step 6: Entity & Attribute Mapping

For the central entity and each major sub-entity:

1. List ALL attributes
2. Prioritize using: **Unique → Root → Rare**
   - Unique attributes = only this entity has them (highest value)
   - Root attributes = fundamental characteristics
   - Rare attributes = uncommon but differentiating
3. Score with **PPR: Prominence, Popularity, Relevance**
   - Prominence = how visible/important is this attribute?
   - Popularity = how often do users search for it?
   - Relevance = how closely does it relate to the central entity?

### Step 7: Frame Semantics Coverage Check

For EVERY topic in the map, verify these six semantic slots are planned. Google has a "semantic frame" for every topic — expected slots that must be filled for a site to be considered expert.

| Slot | Question | Content Type |
|------|----------|-------------|
| Identity | What is it? | Definition, explanation |
| Mechanism | How does it work? | Process, methodology |
| Users | Who uses it? | Audience, use cases |
| Selection | How do you choose? | Comparison, criteria |
| Problems | What can go wrong? | Troubleshooting, pitfalls |
| Outcomes | What results? | Case studies, benchmarks |

**Miss a slot = semantic gap = "This site doesn't fully understand this topic."**

### Step 8: Authority Gap Analysis

Research competitors and identify:
- **Depth gaps** — themes where competitors have surface coverage but no comprehensive guides
- **BOFU gaps** — high-intent buyer keywords with low competition (comparison pages, alternative pages, product-led content)
- **Bridge topics** — subjects that connect the product/service to specific buyer needs
- **Missing frame slots** — semantic slots competitors haven't filled

Use Ahrefs, SERP analysis, and competitor content audits when tools are available.

### Step 9: Content Intent Distribution

Plan the content mix across the funnel. The most common mistake is 80% TOFU / 15% MOFU / 5% BOFU — optimizing for traffic instead of pipeline.

| Intent Stage | Target % | Content Types |
|-------------|----------|---------------|
| TOFU | 30-40% | Educational guides, "what is" posts, industry explainers |
| MOFU | 30-40% | Comparison guides, "how to choose", use-case content |
| BOFU | 20-30% | "[Brand] vs [Competitor]", alternative pages, product-led content, case studies |

BOFU content converts at 10x the rate of TOFU. Prioritize it.

### Step 10: Pillar-Cluster Architecture

Design the pillar-cluster structure:

- **Pillar pages** = comprehensive guides (4,000–5,000 words), definitive resources on a parent theme
- **Cluster pages** = focused articles covering specific sub-topics (1,500–3,000 words)
- Each pillar links to/from **8–14 cluster documents**
- Every cluster links back to its pillar AND to relevant sibling clusters
- **Zero orphan pages** — every page must be connected
- URL structure reflects the topical hierarchy

## Output Format

Produce a structured topical map document with:

1. **Source Context & Central Entity** — brand identity, business model, central entity, central search intent
2. **Complete Topic Hierarchy** — full tree with Core sections, Outer sections, and Contextual Bridges
3. **Query Networks** — query paths, correlative queries, and query templates for each parent theme
4. **Frame Semantics Plan** — coverage matrix showing all 6 slots for each topic
5. **Authority Gap Analysis** — competitor gaps, BOFU opportunities, bridge topics
6. **Pillar-Cluster Architecture** — which pages are pillars, which are clusters, how they connect
7. **Content Priority Order** — what to publish first, second, third (based on gaps + impact)
8. **Intent Distribution** — TOFU/MOFU/BOFU breakdown with specific content pieces assigned

---

## Research Approach

When building a topical map, use every available research tool:
- **Web search** for competitor analysis and SERP review
- **Ahrefs MCP** for keyword data, competitor organic keywords, content gaps
- **Google Search Console MCP** for existing performance data
- **SERP analysis** for People Also Ask, featured snippets, AI overviews
- **NotebookLM** for any stored research or course material

Build evidence-based maps, not assumption-based ones.
